Ball Rolling
60–90 sec · both feet · arch only
Roll the arch only. Do not roll over the heel. Stop immediately if you feel sharp pain.
Time
60–90s
Feet
Both
Kit
Tennis ball

Seated, place a tennis ball under your arch. Apply moderate pressure and roll slowly along the arch. Uncomfortable is fine, sharp pain is not. Don't stand on the ball.

Lie on your side, hips stacked, knees bent, feet together. Rotate the top knee upward like a clamshell opening. Don't let the pelvis roll back. Lower slowly. Targets the glute medius, which directly affects lower limb mechanics when running.

On Hold

Confirm with physio first
Knee to Wall
Ankle dorsiflexion mobility
On hold
Do not start until your physio confirms it's safe. This loads the Achilles insertion into dorsiflexion, which can aggravate insertional tendinopathy.

Stand facing a wall, toes a set distance away. Push the knee forward to touch the wall, heel flat on the floor. Improves ankle dorsiflexion range. The concern is that this position compresses the Achilles insertion against the heel bone.

Slant Board Isometric Hold
Achilles loading · start 30°
On hold
Do not use until your physio confirms it's safe. Same dorsiflexion concern as knee to wall. Ask about both at the same time.
Start
30°
Sets
3
Hold
45s
Progress
+5° / 2wk

Stand on the slant board at 30 degrees with a slight knee bend and hold. No movement. Isometric loading builds tendon tolerance before dynamic work. Progress to 35 degrees after 2 weeks if pain free, then 40, 45, 50.

Stop any exercise that reproduces sharp or stabbing pain. If your right foot goes numb during a run, stop and wiggle or rotate the foot before continuing. If symptoms worsen, rest and contact your physio.
